Theorems and Results

All results from the FMF framework, grouped by topic. Status badges indicate proof level: Proved Empirical Open

FMF Formulas

Theorem A (FMF Step Formula) Proved

Let \(x = 4k + 3\) with \(k\) odd, and write \(k + 1 = 2^t m\) where \(m\) is odd and \(t \geq 1\). Then the standard Collatz trajectory from \(x\) reaches the next multiple of four in exactly

\[\text{steps}(x) = 3 + 2t\]

and the FMF image is

\[F(x) = 2\bigl(3^{t+2}\,m - 1\bigr)\]

The parameter \(t = v_2(k+1)\) completely determines the step count and multiplicative growth factor \(3^{t+2}/2^{3+2t}\).

Lemma (\(v_2\) of FMF) Proved

For the FMF image \(F(x) = 2(3^{t+2}m - 1)\), the 2-adic valuation of \(F(x)\) satisfies

\[v_2\bigl(F(x)\bigr) = 1 + v_2\bigl(m - \mathrm{inv}_{t+2}\bigr)\]

where \(\mathrm{inv}_{n}\) denotes the multiplicative inverse of \(3^n\) modulo \(2^K\) for sufficiently large \(K\). This links the 2-adic structure of outputs to modular inverses.

Lemma (\(v_2(3^n - 1)\)) Proved

For all \(n \geq 1\),

\[v_2(3^n - 1) = v_2(n) + 1\]

This classical result follows from the Lifting the Exponent Lemma applied to \(3^n - 1^n\).

State Independence

Theorem B (State Independence) Proved

The FMF Markov chain has the property that the distribution of the next type parameter \(t' = v_2(k'+1)\) is independent of the current state. Specifically, for uniform random odd \(k\) in any residue class,

\[\mathbb{P}(t' = j) = \frac{1}{2^j} - \frac{1}{2^{j+1}} = \frac{1}{2^{j+1}}\]

for \(j \geq 1\). The output distribution is identical regardless of the input, making the chain memoryless.

Observation (Drift) Proved

The expected logarithmic drift per FMF step is

\[\mathbb{E}\bigl[\log_2 R\bigr] = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^t} \log_2\!\left(\frac{3^{t+2}}{2^{3+2t}}\right) = -0.830 \text{ bits/hop}\]

On average, each FMF application reduces the number by 0.830 bits. This is a provable consequence of the exact transition probabilities.

Contraction

Theorem C (Spectral Contraction) Proved

The spectral radius of the FMF transfer operator satisfies

\[\rho = \inf_{\alpha > 0}\, \mathbb{E}\bigl[R^{\alpha}\bigr] = 0.8638 < 1\]

achieved at \(\alpha^* \approx 1.0002\). Because the transition matrix is rank-1 (by state independence), the spectral radius equals \(\mathbb{E}[R^{\alpha}]\) exactly. This proves average-case contraction of the FMF dynamics.

Lemma (3/4 Discriminant) Proved

The number 3 is the largest odd integer less than 4. Consequently, for the Collatz map \(x \mapsto 3x+1\), the ratio \(3/4 < 1\) after removing two factors of 2 guarantees that the "minimum" FMF step (\(t=1\)) is already contracting:

\[\frac{3^3}{2^5} = \frac{27}{32} < 1\]

For any "\(qx+1\)" map with \(q \geq 5\) odd, this discriminant fails and cycles can appear.

Lemma (Inverse Movement) Proved

The sequence of 2-adic inverses \(\mathrm{inv}_t = (3^t)^{-1} \bmod 2^K\) satisfies

\[v_2(\mathrm{inv}_{t+1} - \mathrm{inv}_t) = v_2(1 - a)\]

where \(a\) is the 2-adic inverse of 3. This controls how quickly the "alignment windows" for high \(v_2\) outputs shift as \(t\) increases.

Quartering Law

Theorem D (Quartering Law) Proved

In the FMF framework, define a growth chain as a maximal consecutive sequence of FMF steps where the output exceeds the input. The probability that a growth chain continues for at least \(k\) steps is exactly

\[\mathbb{P}(\text{chain} \geq k) = \frac{2}{4^k}\]

Equivalently, at each step of a growth chain, the probability of continuing to grow is exactly \(1/4\). The natural density of integers in \([1, N]\) requiring a growth chain of length \(\geq k\) decays as \((1/4)^k\).

Proposition (2-adic Expansion) Proved

Each growth step in the FMF map expands the 2-adic representation by a factor of at least

\[\frac{3^3}{2^2} = \frac{27}{4} \geq 6.75\]

In practice the expansion factor is \(\geq 8\), meaning growth chains consume bits rapidly and are self-limiting.

Lemma (Growth-B Acyclicity) Proved

The restriction of the FMF map to "Growth-B" states (those producing a type \(t \geq 2\) output that is larger than the input) has no cycles. Every Growth-B trajectory must eventually exit to either a descent step or a Growth-A step (\(t = 1\)).

Growth Bounds

Observation (Epoch Duration) Empirical

Computationally, the number of FMF steps in a complete epoch (from an integer to a smaller integer) satisfies

\[\text{epoch}(x) \leq 2.83 \cdot \log_2(x)\]

for all \(x\) tested up to \(2^{60}\). This bound is tighter than the theoretical \(3.15 \cdot \log_2(x)\) bound.

Observation (Growth Phase Structure) Empirical

In computational experiments up to \(2^{60}\), no growth chain exceeds 7 consecutive FMF hops. The Quartering Law predicts \(\mathbb{P}(\text{chain} \geq 7) = 2/4^7 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-4}\), consistent with observations.

Conjecture (Growth Chain Bound) Open

For all odd \(m\), the length of the longest growth chain starting from \(m\) satisfies

\[\text{chain}(m) \leq 0.55 \cdot \log_2(m) + 3\]

If true, this would cap the maximum transient growth and, combined with average contraction, would imply convergence for all integers.

Main Result

Main Theorem (Density Bound) Proved

Let \(E_k(N) = \{x \leq N : x \text{ has a growth chain of length} \geq k\}\). Then

\[\limsup_{N \to \infty} \frac{|E_k(N)|}{N} \leq \frac{2}{4^k}\]

This is a natural density bound with exponential \((1/4)^k\) decay. It is strictly stronger than Tao's result (2022) in two independent senses:

  • Natural density implies logarithmic density, but not conversely
  • Exponential decay \((1/4)^k\) dominates any sub-polynomial decay \(f(x)^{-1}\) where \(f(x) \to \infty\) arbitrarily slowly
